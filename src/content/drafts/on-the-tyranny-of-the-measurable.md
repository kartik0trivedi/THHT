There's a pattern that shows up everywhere. A school adopts a metric. Teachers optimise for it. The metric goes up. The thing the metric was supposed to represent stays flat, or gets worse. Everyone is confused.

This isn't a cynical observation about human nature — it's a structural one. When you make something measurable, you change what it is. The measure becomes the target, and the territory it was supposed to map gets abandoned.

## Goodhart's Law and its discontents

The economist Charles Goodhart said it in 1975: *when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.* It gets cited constantly, usually as a warning that's already been ignored.

What's less discussed is the asymmetry. The things we can measure — test scores, citations, click-through rates, response times — tend to improve under optimization pressure. The things we can't — understanding, quality, trust, health — tend to degrade in the same process. The ratio shifts, gradually, until we've built institutions that are very good at producing numbers and quietly terrible at what those numbers were supposed to represent.

## Why this is hard to fix

The first problem is legibility. If you can't measure something, you can't demonstrate that it's getting worse. So the people making decisions often genuinely don't see the degradation. The signal is weak; the metric is loud.

The second problem is incentives. The measurable things become the currency of careers and budgets. Fighting for the unmeasurable is a hard argument to make in a room full of dashboards.

The third problem is feedback loops. Once you've selected for people who are good at optimising the metric, you've filtered out — or trained away — the people who would have pushed back against it.

---

I don't have a clean answer to this. But I think the first step is to be honest about what we're not measuring, and to hold that awareness alongside the dashboards, rather than letting the dashboards eat it.

That requires a different kind of discipline — the discipline of not optimising, of sitting with ambiguity, of trusting things that don't show up in the numbers. That's uncomfortable. It's also, I think, where most of the real work is.
